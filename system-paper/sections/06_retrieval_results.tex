\section{Retrieval Evaluation}
\label{sec:retrieval-results}

Having seen that retrieval barely moves end-to-end quality
(\S\ref{sec:endtoend}), we now isolate the retriever to ask whether the
problem is \emph{what} is retrieved. We score it on mamaretrieval---3{,}185
clinical queries with graded ($0$--$6$) relevance labels over the deployment
corpus, judged by Qwen3.5-397B and validated against Claude~Opus~4.7
(construction in the companion paper)---at the deployed retrieval depth of
top-3.

Because all three retrieved chunks are injected into the prompt together, the
operationally meaningful questions are whether any useful chunk is present
(Hit Rate) and how much of the small top-3 bundle is useful rather than noise
(Precision); rank-sensitive metrics such as MRR or nDCG would matter only if
order within the top-3 strongly shaped the answer, which we do not assume. We
report Hit Rate and Precision at a lenient threshold (relevance $\ge 3$, any
useful content) and a strict one ($\ge 5$, complete/specific content),
counting queries with no qualifying chunk as Hit Rate $=$ Precision $= 0$, as
a user would experience them.

We also report threshold-free weighted variants (wHR, wP) that replace the
binary relevance test with each chunk's graded score normalised to $[0,1]$
(the $0$--$6$ grade divided by $6$, so a $3$ contributes $0.5$). For each
query, wHR is the highest normalised grade among its top-3 chunks and wP is
the mean normalised grade across the three slots (empty slots counting $0$);
both are averaged over queries---the graded analogues of Hit Rate (best chunk
present) and Precision (average bundle quality).

The deployed retriever lands in the top tier---third of seven, between two
cloud embedders, within $\sim$8 points of precision of the best (voyage). A
300M model running on the phone competes with cloud retrieval, and it
improves on the earlier on-device Gecko by $+30.7$ points of lenient
precision (0.784 vs.\ 0.477), closing essentially the whole on-device gap.
One honest caveat sits in the strict Hit Rate column of
Table~\ref{tab:retrievers}: even at the top it is far below the lenient
one---voyage carries a complete, specific ($\ge5$) chunk in its top-3 for
only $\sim$75\% of queries ($\text{HR}=0.753$), and the deployed
EmbeddingGemma for $\sim$70\% ($0.704$). At the deployed depth, then, a
meaningful minority of queries have no fully specific chunk in the bundle---a
gap that deeper retrieval or a broader corpus, rather than a better top-3
embedder, would have to close.

\subsection{A better embedder does not reach the answers}

If retrieval quality were the constraint, the Gecko$\to$EmbeddingGemma
upgrade should lift end-to-end answers. It does not (Table~\ref{tab:bakeoff}).
On a matched comparison, the upgrade raises retrieval precision sharply
($0.270\to0.396$, $+12.6$ points) while end-to-end key-fact recall stays flat
($0.125\to0.126$) and the HealthBench score barely moves. More starkly, on
this small generator \emph{no retrieval at all} scores highest on Kenya
(0.178)---adding context, however good, does not help and slightly hurts.

Why do the retrieval gains not convert? The information is not missing:
precision rises, so it is retrieved, and the corpus contains it and it cannot
be gated away (\S\ref{sec:retr-corpus}, \S\ref{sec:retr-gate}). The break is
downstream, in the small generator's response behaviour---which the prompt
matrix (\S\ref{sec:endtoend}) already exposed and which better context does
not change. It declines to engage on a large share of questions, and even
when it engages it tends to withhold specific guidance, deferring to ``see a
doctor''; for a deflecting or non-committal answer, no improvement in the
retrieved chunk can raise its key-fact recall. (A subtler, metric-level
contributor: retrieval precision rewards \emph{topical} relevance, which need
not coincide with the specific gold facts a key-fact-recall answer is scored
on.) We isolate the generator's use of context directly in
\S\ref{sec:generator}.

One bound on this result: the bake-off uses the \emph{baseline} prompt and
predates the deployed G1 prompt, so the deflection above is the baseline's
33\%. Because G1 cuts deflection to 3\% (\S\ref{sec:endtoend}), whether a
better embedder reaches answers once the model engages is a separate
question, which a G1 replication settles (\S\ref{sec:retr-g1}). The broader
claim---that the generator, not retrieval, is the binding constraint---rests
also on the prompt-independent faithfulness analysis (\S\ref{sec:generator}).

\subsection{Reranking: an offline win that does not transfer}

A fine-tuned reranker makes the same point from a second angle. Re-scoring a
first stage's \emph{top-20} candidates with a tiny 23M MiniLM-L6
cross-encoder (fine-tuned on 159k in-domain pairs, shipping as an int8
on-device model) and keeping the top-3 lifts precision on the benchmark
dramatically---from 0.485 to 0.72, and a larger fine-tuned variant to 0.79
(Table~\ref{tab:rerank}). Yet none of that reaches the deployment queries:
across rerankers \emph{and} across all three first stages tested (Gecko,
BM25, and their hybrid), Kenya retrieval precision stays near 0.27 and
end-to-end key-fact recall near 0.12, with HealthBench at zero. The benchmark
gain does not transfer to the Kenyan vignettes---a distribution shift with a
methodological lesson: retriever changes must be validated on the deployment
queries, not on the benchmark alone. We do not ship the reranker.

\subsection{It is not the corpus}
\label{sec:retr-corpus}

Nor is the corpus the gap. A coverage audit finds a relevant chunk present
for $\sim$96\% of Kenya queries at the lenient bar and $\sim$89\% at the
strict bar; only $\sim$11\% are genuinely absent. The chain
retrieval~$\to$~corpus is therefore intact, and the answer breaks at the last
step---the generator's use of what it is handed.

\subsection{No usable confidence gate}
\label{sec:retr-gate}

A natural mitigation for ``RAG sometimes hurts'' is an abstention gate:
detect a weak retrieval and fall back to no-RAG. The headroom is real---a
perfect per-query oracle gate would lift Kenya recall $0.297\to0.354$---but no
available signal realises it. EmbeddingGemma's cosine similarity separates
relevant from irrelevant chunks only moderately (AUC $0.72$, below the $0.80$
viability bar), and against a $\sim$20\% relevant base rate no cutoff yields
both decent precision and recall (Table~\ref{tab:gate}): even keeping only
the top-scoring 11\% of chunks, strict precision is just 0.41.

Decisively, the retrieval score has \emph{no} relationship to answer quality:
it predicts whether RAG helps a query at AUC $0.50$ (chance; Pearson
$-0.04$), and a fine-tuned reranker score does no better. All three
threshold-rule families---absolute cutoff, relative margin, gated
abstention---fall below the viability bar. A usable confidence signal, like
the answer-quality fix itself, would have to come from the generator, not
from retrieval.

\medskip
Every retrieval lever points the same way. The deployed retriever is strong
and competes with the cloud; a better embedder and a fine-tuned reranker both
win offline; the corpus covers the questions; and no confidence gate can
sidestep the bad cases---yet end-to-end answers do not move. The binding
constraint is the small on-device generator's ability to turn retrieved
context into better answers, which we examine directly next
(\S\ref{sec:generator}) and whose remedy---grounding the generator---we take
up in the discussion (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).
