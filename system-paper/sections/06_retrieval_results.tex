\section{Retrieval Evaluation}
\label{sec:retrieval-results}

Having seen that retrieval barely moves end-to-end quality
(\S\ref{sec:endtoend}), we now isolate the retriever to ask whether the
problem is \emph{what} is retrieved. We score it on mamaretrieval---3{,}185
clinical queries with graded ($0$--$6$) relevance labels over the deployment
corpus, judged by Qwen3.5-397B and validated against Claude~Opus~4.7
(construction in the companion paper)---at the deployed retrieval depth of
top-3.

Because all three retrieved chunks are injected into the prompt together, the
operationally meaningful questions are whether any useful chunk is present
(Hit Rate) and how much of the small top-3 bundle is useful rather than noise
(Precision); rank-sensitive metrics such as MRR or nDCG would matter only if
order within the top-3 strongly shaped the answer, which we do not assume. We
report Hit Rate and Precision at a lenient threshold (relevance $\ge 3$, any
useful content) and a strict one ($\ge 5$, complete/specific content),
counting queries with no qualifying chunk as Hit Rate $=$ Precision $= 0$, as
a user would experience them.

We also report threshold-free weighted variants (wHR, wP) that replace the
binary relevance test with each chunk's graded score normalised to $[0,1]$
(the $0$--$6$ grade divided by $6$, so a $3$ contributes $0.5$). For each
query, wHR is the highest normalised grade among its top-3 chunks and wP is
the mean normalised grade across the three slots (empty slots counting $0$);
both are averaged over queries---the graded analogues of Hit Rate (best chunk
present) and Precision (average bundle quality).

\subsection{On-device retrieval is strong}

Figure~\ref{fig:retrievers} scores seven retrievers: the deployed on-device
EmbeddingGemma-300M, the earlier on-device Gecko, three cloud embedders
(voyage-4-large, octen, lateon), and lexical/biomedical baselines (BM25,
MedCPT). EmbeddingGemma was added to the original six-retriever pool in a
matched run under the same judge; the six reproduce exactly, confirming no
drift (companion paper).

The deployed retriever lands in the top tier---third of seven, between two
cloud embedders, within $\sim$8 points of precision of the best (voyage). A
300M model running on the phone competes with cloud retrieval, and it
improves on the earlier on-device Gecko by $+30.7$ points of lenient
precision (0.784 vs.\ 0.477), closing essentially the whole on-device gap.
The threshold-free weighted precision tells the same story (EmbeddingGemma
$\text{wP}=0.619$ vs.\ Gecko $0.393$, voyage $0.682$), as do both Hit-Rate
variants. One honest caveat is in Hit Rate at the strict bar, not in
precision: even the best retriever carries a complete, specific ($\ge5$)
chunk in its top-3 for only $\sim$75\% of queries (voyage $\text{HR}=0.753$;
deployed EmbeddingGemma $0.704$). At the deployed depth, then, a meaningful
minority of queries have no fully specific chunk in the bundle---a gap that
deeper retrieval or a broader corpus, rather than a better top-3 embedder,
would have to close.

\subsection{A better embedder does not reach the answers}

If retrieval quality were the constraint, the Gecko$\to$EmbeddingGemma
upgrade should lift end-to-end answers. It does not (Table~\ref{tab:bakeoff}).
On a matched comparison, the upgrade raises retrieval precision sharply
($0.270\to0.396$, $+12.6$ points) while end-to-end key-fact recall stays flat
($0.125\to0.126$) and the HealthBench score barely moves. More starkly, on
this small generator \emph{no retrieval at all} scores highest on Kenya
(0.178)---adding context, however good, does not help and slightly hurts.

Why do the retrieval gains not convert? The information is not missing:
precision rises, so it is retrieved, and the corpus contains it and it cannot
be gated away (\S\ref{sec:retr-corpus}, \S\ref{sec:retr-gate}). The break is
downstream, in the small generator's response behaviour---which the prompt
matrix (\S\ref{sec:endtoend}) already exposed and which better context does
not change. It declines to engage on a large share of questions, and even
when it engages it tends to withhold specific guidance, deferring to ``see a
doctor''; for a deflecting or non-committal answer, no improvement in the
retrieved chunk can raise its key-fact recall. (A subtler, metric-level
contributor: retrieval precision rewards \emph{topical} relevance, which need
not coincide with the specific gold facts a key-fact-recall answer is scored
on.) We isolate the generator's use of context directly in
\S\ref{sec:generator}.

One apparent bound dissolves on inspection. The bake-off above uses the
\emph{baseline} prompt, whose 33\% deflection could be masking a retrieval
gain that a more engaged model would show. It is not: re-running all three
arms under the deployed G1 prompt, which cuts deflection to 3\%, leaves the
ranking unchanged (\S\ref{sec:retr-g1} below). The broader claim---that the
generator, not retrieval, is the binding constraint---rests also on the
prompt-independent faithfulness analysis (\S\ref{sec:generator}).

\subsection{The same holds under the deployed G1 prompt}
\label{sec:retr-g1}

Re-running the bake-off under the deployed Gemma~4\,+\,G1 settles the
deflection objection (Table~\ref{tab:bakeoff-g1}). With deflection fixed at
3\%, the ranking is unchanged: \emph{no retrieval still leads} on Kenya
(recall 0.328), and both retrievers sit below it (EmbeddingGemma 0.279, Gecko
0.268). The better embedder again edges Gecko---$+0.011$ Kenya recall,
$+0.009$ HealthBench \texttt{weighted\_met}---but does not reach the no-RAG
baseline. So the earlier finding was not an artefact of the baseline's
deflection: even when the model engages, retrieved context does not raise
answer quality on this small generator, and slightly lowers it. The size
ladder of \S\ref{sec:model-size} shows the same effect deepening with
capability---the RAG penalty grows from $-0.01$ HealthBench at 4B to $-0.10$
at 31B---consistent with the reading that the generator's parametric knowledge
already exceeds what the retrieved context adds.

\subsection{Reranking: an offline win that does not transfer}

A reranker is a second stage over retrieval: a \emph{cross-encoder} that
reads each (query, chunk) pair jointly and scores its relevance. It is more
accurate than the bi-encoder retriever---which embeds query and chunk
separately---but too costly to run over the whole corpus, so it only
re-scores the first stage's top-20 candidates and re-picks the top-3. We
screened several deployable cross-encoders (MiniLM-L6 at 23M, ELECTRA-base,
bge-reranker-base, mxbai-base) against multi-billion-parameter Qwen3-Reranker
references, and fine-tuned the smallest, MiniLM-L6, in-domain: a regression
onto the mamaretrieval judge's normalised relevance grade ($\text{grade}/6$)
over 158{,}993 train-split (query, chunk) pairs, two epochs, evaluated on
held-out test queries. \emph{Fine-tuning, not size, was the lever}: the 23M
fine-tuned model reached benchmark P@3 0.756, beating the $8\times$-larger
zero-shot mxbai-base (0.714) and approaching the multi-billion-parameter
references (which it exceeds at the strict cut); its int8 on-device build
reproduces the offline scores exactly (top-3 agreement 1.000).

That win does not reach the user. Re-scoring the first stage's top-20 with the
fine-tuned reranker lifts benchmark precision dramatically---from 0.485 to
0.72, and the larger fine-tuned variant to 0.79 (Table~\ref{tab:rerank}). Yet
across rerankers \emph{and} across all three first stages tested (Gecko,
BM25, and their hybrid), Kenya retrieval precision stays near 0.27, end-to-end
key-fact recall near 0.12, and HealthBench completeness near 0.18 against a
flat 0.37 penalty (net \texttt{weighted\_met} $\approx0$). The benchmark
gain does not transfer to the Kenyan vignettes---a distribution shift with a
methodological lesson: retriever changes must be validated on the deployment
queries, not on the benchmark alone. We do not ship the reranker.

\subsection{It is not the corpus}
\label{sec:retr-corpus}

Nor is the corpus the gap. A coverage audit finds a relevant chunk present
for $\sim$96\% of Kenya queries at the lenient bar and $\sim$89\% at the
strict bar; only $\sim$11\% are genuinely absent. The chain
retrieval~$\to$~corpus is therefore intact, and the answer breaks at the last
step---the generator's use of what it is handed.

\subsection{No usable confidence gate}
\label{sec:retr-gate}

A natural mitigation for ``RAG sometimes hurts'' is an abstention gate:
detect a weak retrieval from its score and fall back to no-RAG. We re-asked
this for the deployed EmbeddingGemma---an earlier study had shelved it for
Gecko---and the verdict is the same: the retrieval score is not thresholdable
into a usable gate. Every relevance signal a gate could key on falls below the
standard $0.80$ AUC viability bar (Figure~\ref{fig:gate-auc}). Cosine
separates relevant from irrelevant Kenya chunks at AUC only $0.72$, and the
three threshold-rule families a gate could implement---an absolute cutoff on
chunk relevance, the top-1 chunk's margin over the rest of the bundle, and
gated abstention on whether the bundle holds any relevant chunk---sit at
$0.69$--$0.72$, with the relative-margin rule near chance. These are pure
retrieval-relevance statistics, computed on EmbeddingGemma's top-3 against
Qwen-judged chunk relevance, so they are independent of the generator and
prompt and hold for the deployed configuration.

A separate, stronger test asks not whether the score finds relevant chunks but
whether it predicts when RAG actually \emph{helps the answer}: it does not---%
AUC $0.50$, Pearson $-0.04$, pure chance---and a perfect oracle gate would lift
recall only $0.297\to0.354$. This answer-side test, however, was run on
Gemma~3n under the baseline prompt ($n=300$), not the deployed
Gemma~4\,+\,G1, so---like the bake-off (\S\ref{sec:retr-g1})---it awaits
confirmation under the shipped configuration; the relevance-side verdict above
does not depend on it. Either way, a usable confidence signal would have to
come from the generator (answer-side confidence or self-consistency), not from
the retrieval score.

\medskip
Every retrieval lever points the same way. The deployed retriever is strong
and competes with the cloud; a better embedder and a fine-tuned reranker both
win offline; the corpus covers the questions; and no confidence gate can
sidestep the bad cases---yet end-to-end answers do not move. The binding
constraint is the small on-device generator's ability to turn retrieved
context into better answers, which we examine directly next
(\S\ref{sec:generator}) and whose remedy---grounding the generator---we take
up in the discussion (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).
