\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Maternal and newborn mortality remain among the highest in sub-Saharan
Africa, and Tanzania---which includes the semi-autonomous archipelago of
Zanzibar---is among ten countries that together account for more than 60\% of
maternal and neonatal deaths worldwide~\cite{bohle2025mamai}. In Zanzibar most
women deliver in government facilities that are often understaffed and
under-resourced, where midwifery care is provided by nurses who lack
midwifery training to international standards~\cite{bohle2025mamai}.
Strengthening midwife-led care is high-leverage---universal access to it
could prevent an estimated 67\% of maternal and 64\% of neonatal
deaths~\cite{nove2021midwives}---yet a basic obstacle is that authoritative,
evidence-based guidance is scarce at the point of care~\cite{swisstph2024mamai}:
the guidelines are long, and looking something up online is unreliable where
connectivity is intermittent and mobile data costly.

We present MAM-AI, a medical question-answering assistant that runs entirely
on an Android device. The nurse-midwife's question is embedded and matched
against an on-device store of guideline passages; the most relevant passages
are added to the prompt; and an on-device language model generates an answer
grounded in those passages, with citations to the source
(Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}; Section~\ref{sec:system-design}). Because every
stage runs locally, the system needs no connectivity after a one-time setup,
and no patient-related query leaves the device. The generator and embedder
are off-the-shelf open models; our contribution is the system around them,
the curated knowledge base, and the evaluation that determines how it should
be configured.

Safety is the governing constraint throughout---wrong advice can cause direct
harm---and we are explicit that MAM-AI is a thoroughly evaluated research
prototype, not a fielded product: a planned field test could not proceed this
cycle, so we have no data on how the system performs in real clinical use;
all our evidence comes from benchmarks and LLM-judge scoring.

The evaluation is layered---end-to-end, then retriever and generator in
isolation, then latency---and yields three findings that structure the paper:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A redesigned system prompt is a major lever on answer
  quality.} The base model deflected (``see a doctor'') on about a third of
  questions; a redesigned prompt cut that to roughly 3\% and about doubled the
  correct guidance that answers carry, with no increase in unsafe answers.

  \item \textbf{On-device retrieval is strong, but it does not reach the
  answers.} A 300M on-device embedder retrieves nearly as well as cloud
  systems---third of seven retrievers, within a few points of the best---yet
  adding its context to the small generator is net-neutral to slightly
  negative. Since the retriever is already strong, the bottleneck lies
  downstream of ranking: in how well the small generator uses retrieved
  context, and in the corpus's coverage and quality for the question at
  hand---not in retrieval itself.

  \item \textbf{The generator matters most, and faithfulness decides which
  model we deploy.} Model choice dominates the results---an unconstrained
  frontier model far outperforms the on-device options---and between the two
  same-size on-device candidates we choose on faithfulness: we deploy the one
  that is as faithful to its sources as the frontier model and about twice as
  faithful as the other, more-helpful but less safe, candidate. Deployment is
  thus a safety-versus-usefulness choice.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A deployable on-device medical RAG system for nurse-midwives in a
  low-connectivity setting, running fully offline on commodity Android
  hardware (Section~\ref{sec:system-design}).
  \item A layered evaluation---end-to-end, retriever, generator, and
  latency---on two purpose-made benchmarks and a validated LLM judge, applied
  to the exact deployed configuration, which yields the three findings above
  (Sections~\ref{sec:methodology}--\ref{sec:latency}).
\end{itemize}

The system, knowledge-base pipeline, benchmarks, and evaluation harness are
open source, and a browser demo of the deployed configuration is publicly
available.\footnote{App and pipeline: \url{https://github.com/nmrenyi/mamai},
\url{https://github.com/nmrenyi/mamai-medical-guidelines}. Live web demo:
\url{https://nmrenyi-mamai-demo.hf.space}. The benchmarks are documented in a
companion paper.}
