\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Maternal and newborn mortality remain among the highest in sub-Saharan
Africa, and Tanzania---which includes the semi-autonomous archipelago of
Zanzibar---is among ten countries that together account for more than 60\% of
maternal and neonatal deaths worldwide~\cite{bohle2025mamai}. In Zanzibar most
women deliver in government facilities that are often understaffed and
under-resourced, where midwifery care is provided by nurses who lack
midwifery training to international standards~\cite{bohle2025mamai}.
Strengthening midwife-led care is high-leverage---universal access to it
could prevent an estimated 67\% of maternal and 64\% of neonatal
deaths~\cite{nove2021midwives}---yet a basic obstacle is that authoritative,
evidence-based guidance is scarce at the point of care~\cite{swisstph2024mamai}:
the guidelines are long, and looking something up online is unreliable where
connectivity is intermittent and mobile data costly.

We present MAM-AI, a medical question-answering assistant that runs entirely
on an Android phone. The nurse-midwife's question is embedded and matched
against an on-device store of guideline passages; the most relevant passages
are added to the prompt; and an on-device language model generates an answer
grounded in those passages, with citations to the source
(Section~\ref{sec:system-design}). Because every stage runs locally, the
system needs no connectivity after a one-time setup, and no patient-related
query leaves the device. The generator and embedder are off-the-shelf open
models; our contribution is the system around them, the curated knowledge
base, and the evaluation that determines how it should be configured.

Safety is the governing constraint throughout---wrong advice can cause direct
harm---and we are explicit that MAM-AI is a thoroughly evaluated research
prototype, not a fielded product: a planned field test could not proceed this
cycle, so we report no point-of-care outcomes. The evaluation is layered
(end-to-end, then retriever and generator in isolation, then latency) and
yields three findings, against the intuitive story, that structure the paper.
\emph{First}, a redesigned system prompt is the largest single lever on
quality: the base model deflected (``see a doctor'') on about a third of
questions, and the redesign cut that to roughly 3\% while about doubling the
correct guidance answers carry, with no increase in unsafe answers.
\emph{Second}, on-device retrieval is largely solved---a 300M on-device
embedder ranks third of seven retrievers, within a few points of cloud
systems---yet better retrieval does not reach the answers: on the small
generator, adding retrieved context is net-neutral to slightly negative, so
the binding constraint is the generator. \emph{Third}, the deciding axis
between candidate on-device models is faithfulness, on which the deployed
model matches an unconstrained frontier model and roughly doubles a
more-helpful but unsafe same-size alternative; deployment is thus a
safety-versus-usefulness choice, resolved toward safety and recovered toward
usefulness by the prompt.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A deployable on-device medical RAG system for nurse-midwives in a
  low-connectivity setting, running fully offline on commodity Android
  hardware (Section~\ref{sec:system-design}).
  \item A layered evaluation---end-to-end, retriever, generator, latency---on
  two purpose-made benchmarks and a validated LLM judge, applied to the exact
  deployed configuration (Sections~\ref{sec:methodology}--\ref{sec:latency}).
  \item Findings that localize the failure mode: a prompt redesign that fixes
  over-deflection; evidence that on-device retrieval is solved but does not
  translate into better answers; and a faithfulness result showing the
  deployed small model matches a frontier model, so generator-side grounding,
  not retrieval or prompting, is the open problem.
  \item An on-device latency characterization on real hardware, including a
  root-caused FP16-GPU decoding failure beyond roughly 5{,}000 tokens of
  context that sets the deployed context ceiling (Section~\ref{sec:latency}).
\end{itemize}

The system, knowledge-base pipeline, benchmarks, and evaluation harness are
open source, and a browser demo of the deployed configuration is publicly
available.\footnote{App and pipeline: \url{https://github.com/nmrenyi/mamai},
\url{https://github.com/nmrenyi/mamai-medical-guidelines}. Live web demo:
\url{https://nmrenyi-mamai-demo.hf.space}. The benchmarks are documented in a
companion paper.}
