\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

In Zanzibar, maternal and newborn mortality remain a serious burden, falling
hardest on rural households. The nurse-midwives who staff government health
facilities carry much of this caseload, often with limited resources and
little specialist backup, and they regularly face complex obstetric,
neonatal, and reproductive-health presentations. Authoritative clinical
guidance for these cases exists---in WHO, national, and reference
guidelines---but it is rarely available at the point of care: the documents
are long, and looking something up online is unreliable where connectivity
is intermittent and mobile data is costly.

We present MAM-AI, a medical question-answering assistant that runs entirely
on an Android phone. The nurse-midwife's question is embedded and matched
against an on-device store of guideline passages; the most relevant passages
are added to the prompt; and an on-device language model generates an
answer grounded in those passages, with citations back to the source
(Section~\ref{sec:system-design}). Embedding, retrieval, and generation all
run locally, so the system needs no connectivity after a one-time setup
download, and no patient-related query ever leaves the device. The two model
components---a generator and an embedder---are off-the-shelf open models;
what we contribute is the system around them, the curated clinical knowledge
base, and the evaluation that determines how it should be configured.

Because wrong medical advice can cause direct patient harm, safety is the
governing constraint throughout, ahead of helpfulness or features. We are
deliberate about what this paper does and does not establish: MAM-AI is a
thoroughly evaluated research prototype, not a fielded product. A planned
field test with nurse-midwives could not proceed this cycle, so we report no
point-of-care outcomes; the evaluation rests on benchmarks and
LLM-as-judge measurements, which we validate but which are not a substitute
for clinical use.

The evaluation is organized in layers---the end-to-end system first, then
the retriever and generator in isolation, then on-device latency---and it
yields a set of findings that run against the intuitive story. We summarize
them here because they also structure the paper. \emph{First}, a redesigned
system prompt is the single largest lever on end-to-end quality: the base
model deflected on roughly a third of questions (``see a doctor'' instead of
guidance), and the redesign cut that to about 3\% while roughly doubling how
much correct guidance answers carry, with no increase in unsafe answers.
\emph{Second}, on-device retrieval is largely a solved problem---a 300M
on-device embedder places third of seven retrievers on our benchmark, within
a few points of cloud systems---yet better retrieval does not reach the
answers: on the small on-device generator, adding retrieved context is
net-neutral to slightly negative. The binding constraint is the generator's
ability to use context, not the quality of what is retrieved. \emph{Third},
the deciding axis between candidate on-device models is faithfulness, and on
that axis the deployed model is as faithful as an unconstrained frontier
model and about twice as faithful as a same-size alternative that is more
helpful but produces genuinely dangerous advice; the deployment is therefore
a safety-versus-usefulness choice, resolved in favour of safety and then
recovered toward usefulness by the prompt.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A deployable on-device medical RAG system for nurse-midwives in a
  low-connectivity setting, with every component running offline on
  commodity Android hardware (Section~\ref{sec:system-design}).
  \item A layered evaluation---end-to-end, retriever, generator, and
  latency---built on two purpose-made benchmarks and a validated LLM judge,
  applied to the exact deployed configuration
  (Sections~\ref{sec:methodology}--\ref{sec:latency}).
  \item Findings that localize the system's failure mode: a prompt redesign
  that fixes over-deflection; evidence that on-device retrieval is solved but
  does not translate into better answers; and a faithfulness result showing
  the deployed small model matches a frontier model and that generator-side
  grounding, not retrieval or prompting, is the open problem.
  \item An on-device latency characterization on real hardware, including a
  root-caused FP16-GPU decoding failure beyond roughly 5{,}000 tokens of
  context that sets the deployed context ceiling (Section~\ref{sec:latency}).
\end{itemize}

open source, and a browser demo of the deployed configuration is publicly
