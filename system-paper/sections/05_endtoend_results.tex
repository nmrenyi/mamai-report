\section{End-to-End Evaluation}
\label{sec:endtoend}

This section evaluates the whole system as a clinician would meet it. To
place the deployed choice in context we run a $3\times3$ matrix: the two
deployable on-device generators (Gemma~4~E4B and Gemma~3n~E4B) plus an
unconstrained frontier model (Qwen3.5-397B) as a capability ceiling, each
under three prompts---the plain baseline, \mbox{+G1} (the
deflection/scope-fix prompt of \S\ref{sec:system-prompt}), and \mbox{+G1+G2}
(G1 plus a consultation-workflow structure). Every cell uses the deployed
RAG pipeline (EmbeddingGemma top-3, bundle v0.3.0) and is scored on the two
open-ended tracks---Kenya vignettes and HealthBench-oss---by the validated
gpt-oss-120b judge. We score on these open-ended tracks because they match
the deployment task; multiple-choice is only a control, and there adding RAG
slightly \emph{lowers} accuracy (53.7\%~$\to$~51.9\% over 23{,}241 items)
because the maternal corpus is off-topic for broad USMLE-style questions.
Table~\ref{tab:matrix} is the result; the same matrix under oracle context,
which isolates faithfulness, is analysed in \S\ref{sec:generator}.

\subsection{The prompt fixes deflection on the deployed model}

The baseline Gemma~4 is \emph{safe but unhelpful}: it deflects on 32.7\% of
Kenya questions---answering ``see a doctor'' instead of giving first-line
management---so its key-fact recall is only 0.139 and its HealthBench
\texttt{weighted\_met} is essentially zero. It rarely says anything wrong
because it rarely says anything. The G1 prompt is designed to cure exactly
this reflex, and it does: deflection collapses to 3.2\%, recall roughly
doubles to 0.279, and \texttt{weighted\_met} rises off the floor---at almost
no safety cost (dangerous answers $0\to1$, potentially-harmful
$12.8\to15.7\%$). Adding the
G1+G2 consultation structure lifts recall further to 0.338. This is the
single largest end-to-end gain in the study, and it is why G1 ships.

\subsection{Helpfulness versus safety: why Gemma 4, not Gemma 3n}
\label{sec:helpful-safe}

The two on-device generators are not ``better versus worse'' but
\emph{helpful versus safe}, and the split is large. Gemma~3n is the more
helpful model---higher recall at every prompt (0.30--0.39 vs.\ 0.14--0.34)
and about twice the HealthBench score---which is why it is tempting to
deploy. But every safety and faithfulness signal favours Gemma~4 by roughly
$2\times$: the potentially-harmful rate (12.8--17.0\% vs.\ 24.4--31.4\%) and,
most sharply, the count of \emph{dangerous} answers---ones that would harm a
patient if followed---which is $0$/$1$/$0$ for Gemma~4 across the three
prompts but $4$/$11$/$15$ for Gemma~3n. We deploy Gemma~4 and recover its
helpfulness with the prompt; choosing the generator decides the safety trade,
and the prompt only moves along it. This safety gap does not surface in
HealthBench, whose \texttt{weighted\_met} is effectively a completeness score:
in Table~\ref{tab:matrix} the positive component rises with model capability
while the penalty component stays flat ($\sim$0.34--0.39) across all nine
cells, so neither generator nor prompt moves active harm on that rubric. The
safety difference shows up only in the targeted Kenya dangerous count and in
oracle faithfulness (\S\ref{sec:generator}).

\subsection{Dangerous-case adjudication}

Because the dangerous count is the decisive axis, we checked whether those
flags are genuine. Every Kenya response the judge flagged as dangerous was
re-examined by a separate frontier model (Claude Opus~4.8) against the item's
gold key facts and standard clinical references (drug doses,
contraindications, recommended management)---a second, automated check. It
found Gemma~3n's flags to be overwhelmingly genuine---$4/4$ (baseline),
$\sim$10/11 (+G1), and $\sim$7/15 (+G1+G2) real, serious errors; of the
remaining flags, $\sim$3 are judge over-flags and the rest borderline. They are mostly order-of-magnitude dose errors, contraindicated
drugs, and unsafe management actions that the deflection-fix prompt elicits
but the model cannot ground (Figure~\ref{fig:cases})---confident, specific,
and wrong.

Gemma~4's single flagged case, by contrast, was assessed as a judge
over-flag---the answer is standard choking first aid (back blows, chest
thrusts, escalation), not a dangerous one---so Gemma~4~+G1 is effectively
free of genuine dangerous answers (Figure~\ref{fig:cases}, last panel).
Across the matrix the judge's errors run in the over-flagging direction
($\sim$3 of 31 flags);
only the flagged cases were re-examined, so these counts bound flagged
errors, not necessarily all errors.

\subsection{Generator capability is the dominant lever---up to a point}
\label{sec:model-size}

To test directly whether generator \emph{capability}---not the prompt, not
retrieval---is what limits quality, we run a size ladder at the deployed G1
prompt with \emph{no} retrieval, which isolates each model's parametric
medical knowledge: Gemma~4 at 2B, 4B (deployed), 26B, and 33B, then Qwen at
28B--397B as a server-class reference, all scored by the same gpt-oss-120b
judge on Kenya and HealthBench (Figure~\ref{fig:size-ladder}). Three things
stand out.

\emph{Quality rises steeply with size, then plateaus near 30B.} Kenya
key-fact recall climbs from 0.27 (2B) to a peak of $\sim$0.47 at 28--36B, and
HealthBench \texttt{weighted\_met} roughly quintuples (from 0.05--0.10 to
0.26--0.27) over the same range---almost all of it added completeness, as the
Positive component climbs while the penalty stays flat
(Figure~\ref{fig:size-ladder}, bottom). Beyond $\sim$30B both flatten and
dip slightly---recall 0.42--0.44 and \texttt{weighted\_met} 0.19--0.23 at
125B--397B---though this dip also crosses a model-generation boundary (the
125B/397B rungs are an older Qwen generation than the 28/36B peak), so we read
it as a plateau rather than a true decline. Either way, the 397B frontier is
\emph{not} better for this task than a 30B model.

\emph{Safety improves monotonically with size.} The Kenya harm rate falls from
0.26 (2B) to 0.16 (deployed 4B) to 0.06--0.09 (26--33B), and deflection
collapses to near zero by 26B. Larger models are simultaneously more helpful
and safer---no trade-off along the size axis, unlike the generator
\emph{choice} of \S\ref{sec:helpful-safe}, where Gemma~3n bought helpfulness at
a safety cost.

\emph{Precision barely matters at the deployed size.} The ladder runs each
model at a deployment-realistic precision---int4 (Q4\_0) for the on-device
2--4B rungs, bf16 or FP8 for the larger server-class rungs---so reading it as
a clean \emph{size} comparison requires precision itself to be a minor factor.
A separate ablation at fixed 4B confirms it (Table~\ref{tab:precision}):
dequantizing from int4 (Q4\_0) to Q8 to BF16 is flat on Kenya (recall
0.328/0.331/0.320) and worth only $+0.017$ on HealthBench. So int4 on-device
deployment sacrifices almost nothing, the ladder's size effect is not an
artifact of the small models being more aggressively quantized, and a server
gains far more from a larger model than from higher precision.

\emph{The prompt is not the lever either---capability decides its sign.} The
same lesson appears from the prompt side in the matrix
(Table~\ref{tab:matrix}): the identical +G1+G2 prompt \emph{hurts} Gemma~3n
(dangerous $4\to15$), is \emph{neutral} on Gemma~4 ($0\to0$), and is
\emph{pure upside} on Qwen3.5-397B---the best cell on every axis at once (recall
$0.295\to0.553$, potentially-harmful down to 4.2\%, dangerous at zero). What
varies is not the prompt but whether the generator can act on a request for
more clinical substance \emph{without} inventing unsupported claims.

Together these sharpen finding~3: the binding lever is generator capability,
not the prompt, the quantization precision, or the retriever. The deployed
4B-int4 is a safe floor; the single biggest available upgrade is a $\sim$30B
server-class model ($\sim$5$\times$ HealthBench, $+0.14$ Kenya recall, harm
halved), with little reason to go past it. On-device, where a larger model is
not an option, the remaining lever is therefore generator-side
RAG-grounding: fine-tuning the small model to do what only the frontier model
currently does---give specific clinical guidance while keeping every claim
grounded in its retrieved sources---rather than further prompt tuning, higher
precision, or retrieval swaps (\S\ref{sec:future}).
